# Glaucoma

Glaucoma is a group of eye conditions that damage the optic nerve, the nerve that
carries visual information from the eye to the brain. The damage is most often
associated with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP), the fluid pressure inside the
eye, although glaucoma can also occur at normal pressure.

Glaucoma is one of the leading causes of irreversible blindness worldwide. In its
early stages it is usually painless and has no symptoms, which is why it is
sometimes called the "silent thief of sight." Peripheral (side) vision is typically
lost first, and central vision is affected only in advanced disease.

Diagnosis combines several measurements. Intraocular pressure is measured with a
test called tonometry. The optic nerve head is examined directly during a dilated
fundus examination and can be imaged with optical coherence tomography (OCT). A
visual field test maps areas of vision loss.

Treatment aims to lower intraocular pressure and slow further damage. First-line
treatment is usually prescription eye drops, such as prostaglandin analogues.
Laser procedures and surgery are options when drops are not enough. Vision already
lost to glaucoma cannot be recovered, so early detection and consistent treatment
are essential.
