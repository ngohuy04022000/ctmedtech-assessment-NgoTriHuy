# Cataract

A cataract is a clouding of the eye's natural lens, which sits behind the iris and
focuses light onto the retina. Cataracts are most commonly related to aging, but can
also result from injury, certain medications such as long-term steroids, or other
medical conditions.

As a cataract develops, the lens becomes progressively cloudy. Typical symptoms
include blurred or hazy vision, increased glare and difficulty with bright lights or
night driving, faded or yellowed colours, and frequent changes in eyeglass
prescription. Cataracts usually progress slowly over years.

Cataracts are diagnosed during an eye examination, including a slit-lamp examination
that lets the clinician view the lens in detail.

There is no medication that reverses a cataract. The definitive treatment is surgery,
in which the cloudy natural lens is removed and replaced with a clear artificial lens
called an intraocular lens (IOL). The most common technique is phacoemulsification,
where the lens is broken up with ultrasound and removed through a small incision.
Cataract surgery is one of the most common and successful surgical procedures, and
most patients recover good vision.
