# Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is a disease that affects the macula, the
central part of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed central vision. AMD
causes loss of central vision while usually sparing peripheral vision, making tasks
such as reading and recognising faces difficult.

There are two main types. Dry (atrophic) AMD is the more common form; it progresses
slowly and is marked by the build-up of deposits called drusen under the retina. Wet
(neovascular) AMD is less common but more aggressive: abnormal new blood vessels grow
beneath the retina and leak fluid or blood, which can cause rapid and severe central
vision loss.

The main risk factors are increasing age, smoking, and family history or genetic
predisposition.

Treatment differs by type. There is no cure for dry AMD, but for intermediate cases
a specific combination of vitamins and minerals (the AREDS formulation) may slow
progression, and lifestyle changes such as stopping smoking are recommended. Wet AMD
is treated with intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (such as ranibizumab or
aflibercept), which can reduce leakage and help preserve vision when started early.
