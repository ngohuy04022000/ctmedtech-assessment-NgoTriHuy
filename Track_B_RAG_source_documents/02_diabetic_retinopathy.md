# Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy is a complication of diabetes in which chronically high blood
sugar damages the small blood vessels of the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at
the back of the eye. It is a leading cause of vision loss among working-age adults.

The condition is graded in stages. Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR)
is the earlier form and is classified as mild, moderate, or severe based on changes
such as microaneurysms and haemorrhages. Proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR)
is the advanced stage, in which fragile new blood vessels grow on the retina and can
bleed or cause retinal detachment. Diabetic macular edema (DME), swelling of the
central retina, can occur at any stage and is a common cause of vision loss.

Diabetic retinopathy often has no early symptoms, so regular screening is critical.
Screening is performed with a dilated fundus examination or with fundus photography,
in which images of the retina are captured and reviewed for signs of disease.

Management starts with good control of blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol.
For advanced disease, treatments include laser photocoagulation, intravitreal
anti-VEGF injections (especially for diabetic macular edema), and surgery in severe
cases.
